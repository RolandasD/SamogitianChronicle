Chapter 4: Storm over the Baltic

1457–1461 — Centralization, the Gotland War, the Lithuanian and Rus’ Campaigns, the Crimean War, and the 1461 Settlements

Prologue: A State of Sinew

On 3 March 1457 the chancery sealed the decrees for a Centralized Monarchical Bureaucracy. Ledgers, musters, tolls, and tides were drawn into one hand. The armies would march the way the clerks wrote: straight, swift, and inevitable.

— Chronicle of Varniai, “On the Year of Papers and Spears”

Centralization gave us reach; fleets would give us presence. With the Royal Navy and Baltic Fleet balanced across Klaipėda and Riga, the court judged that borders were no longer the edge of our ambition but the beginning of our roads.

“If the realm is a blade, let the chancery be its hilt.”
— Sister Agnė of Varniai

1458 — The Gotland War

2 January 1458 Samogitia declared war on Gotland. The island sat on the seam of trade, a cork in the wine-bottle of the Baltic; to rule its roads was to be heard in every Hanse hall. What followed was a clinical demonstration of maneuver on shore and ruthlessness at sea.

Battle of Gotland (27 May 1458)

Dawn found our columns already ashore, drums padded to keep time without warning. The line stepped cleanly into the island interior: 9,810/5,886/3,924 against a single, lonely thousand. The fight was over before a gull could cross the strand. The beach was ours; the wind, too.

Western Baltic Sea Actions (June 1458)

Meanwhile, the sea became a proving ground for the new doctrine: heavies to dictate, lights to pursue, galleys where the water thinned. On 12 June our 7/16/26/0 line harried a light screen—one ship taken as prize, the rest sent under. The next day, 13 June, we scissored a mixed flotilla of galleys and transports; nothing survived the crossfire.

17 June 1458 the war closed with a tidy peace. The island’s harbors learned new signals, and our sailors, new pride.

“The sea is a sword you wield with both hands: harbor and hull.”
— Admiral Mindaugas Olshanski to the Riga dock guilds

1459 — The Lithuanian Campaign

On 6 January 1459 we struck at Lithuania, their banners soon joined by Novgorod and Odoyev. The plan was written in rivers: press along the Neris towards Vilnius, ride the Daugava to Polockas, and let the guns speak at the crossings.

Set-Piece Battles (February–March 1459)

Lithuania’s great field army came on in winter, hoping frost would tire our horses and silence our guns. Instead, the snow carried the sound of our drums further. At Vilnius (6 Feb) we shattered their main, then pivoted to Polockas—three engagements in twenty days, all decided by weight and timing.

1460 — The Rus’ Fronts

1 June 1460 the war standard rose against Pskov, with Muscovy and Perm at their back. We chose speed: strike Ostrov twice before reserves could knit, then turn east across the lakes and roads of the Upper Volga.

Campaign Actions (June–September 1460)

Ostrov broke twice in four days. The road to Moskva opened like a seam; there, our guns outran their powder and scattered the rally. By Rostov in September, the enemy was a coat with too few buttons—every fastening torn away.

1461 — South to the Steppes

30 July 1461 Samogitia declared war on Crimea, joined by Kazan, Nogai, and Ramazan. Grassland favored movement; our horse ran the enemy’s flanks until the guns could walk forward and decide the center.

Poltava (19 August 1461)

At Poltava, the open field became a geometry problem: our cavalry drew the line; our guns provided the angle. The enemy broke in a slow, reluctant spiral—resistance unwound thread by thread until nothing remained but flags and dust.

Peaces and the New Map (1461)

A season of capitals and ink: 11 September 1461 peace with Crimea; 28 September 1461 peace with Pskov; 11 November 1461 peace with Lithuania. The chancery’s script redrew the Baltic world with a cleaner, heavier hand.

Balance of Power, 1461

The campaigns settled three questions. First, the sea: Gotland and the Gulf victories proved we could decide on water. Second, the rivers: Vilnius and Polockas showed that crossings yield to method and nerve. Third, the steppe: at Poltava our cavalry learned to dance with our guns. From Riga to the lower Dnieper, chancelleries began to draft their letters with our shadow in mind.

“The map is not merely where you are; it is what others believe you can reach.”
— Court astrologer’s marginalia, 1461
