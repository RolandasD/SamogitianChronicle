Chapter 3: Lion of the North

1449–1456 — Swedish Independence, Renaissance, the Royal Navy, and the Imperial Crown

1449 — A Promise to Sweden

On 15 November 1449 a messenger from Stockholm reached the court at dusk. Sweden sought friends for a bid at freedom; Samogitia answered before the wax had cooled on the seal. By 1 December 1449, our banners moved with theirs in the War of Swedish Independence.

“Let the Baltic learn that we are not merely watchers of storms. We are the wind.” — Attributed to a court orator in Medininkai, winter 1449

1450 — Three New Standards

The realm did not prepare with speeches alone. By January 1450 three new regiments joined the line, each mustering 5k/3k/2k:

  • Royal Force — Gintautas Tyzenhaus
  • Pagan Fear — Tarvydas Korybut
  • Iron Legion — Lingvenis Olshanski

With Iron Wolf, Black Death, and Green Legion already blooded, Samogitia now carried a true army of maneuver—six standards, one purpose.

1453 — Peace, Renaissance, and a Crown

17 February 1453: Denmark yielded: ten years of reparations and 345 ducats; Samogitia gained +2.0 prestige (Denmark −2.0).

1 March 1453: the court embraced the Renaissance, drawing scholars and surveyors to the capital. 9 March 1453: noble privileges were curtailed, and the crown’s hand steadied upon the tiller. 14 April 1453: the bells out-rang the gulls as our sovereigns were crowned Emperor and Empress of Samogitia.

Armies Recast — August 1453

The veteran corps—Iron Wolf, Black Death, and Green Legion—each expanded to 10k/6k/4k on 18 August, the heartbeat of a new imperial field army.

1454 — The Sea Opens

On January 1454 mass training began across all banners—the old drill made new with Renaissance methods. Then, on 8 May, masts rose in Klaipėda: twelve heavy ships crewed up under Mindaugas Olshanski, and the Royal Navy was born.

The fleet grew with startling speed. Where we had been a land power pressed to the shore, by year’s end the quays counted not just heavies but screens and oar-fleets. Below, the naval ledger tracks every step:

What the Navy Changed

The first twelve heavies gave us a mailed fist; the screens made it swift. Galleys bit into shallows and channels the heavies dared not. And with transports came reach: a promise that Samogitia’s armies would no longer be bounded by bridges.

“A crown is safest when it floats.” — Admiral Mindaugas Olshanski, to the dockyard guilds

1456 — Steel and Oath Renewed

The deaths of captains often write the margins of history. On 20 January 1456, Tarvydas Korybut fell from the rolls; Daugvilas Jogailaitis took command of Pagan Fear. The same year, Royal Force and Iron Legion swelled to 10k/6k/4k, aligning with the great triad of 1453. By autumn the fleet carried three flagships—Death, Fear, and Agony—names to make a harbor quiet.

End-of-year tallies: Army 55,000 / 33,000 / 22,000. Navy 16 / 31 / 51 / 20 (heavy / light / galley / transport).
