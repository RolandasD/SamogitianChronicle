Chapter 3: Lion of the North

1449–1456 — Swedish Independence, Renaissance, the Royal Navy, and the Imperial Crown

Prologue: When the Sea Learned Our Name

They say the gulls of Klaipėda wheeled differently the day our first masts rose. Not for fear—gulls fear nothing—but for surprise: a land realm had found its sea legs. In the logbooks of that spring, a clerk wrote, “The wind is ours as much as theirs.”

— Chronicle of Varniai, “On the Raising of the Royal Navy”

On 15 November 1449, Sweden’s quiet plea reached Medininkai. Before the wax cooled, we gave our word. By 1 December our standards moved with theirs in the War of Swedish Independence. What followed was not a rush of glory so much as a long, deliberate stride—the measured step of a realm deciding what it would be.

“Let the Baltic learn that we are not merely watchers of storms. We are the wind.”
— Attributed to a court orator, winter 1449

1450 — Three New Standards

Preparation came in steel and drill. By January 1450 three new regiments joined the line, each mustering 5k/3k/2k:

  • Royal Force — Gintautas Tyzenhaus
  • Pagan Fear — Tarvydas Korybut
  • Iron Legion — Lingvenis Olshanski

With Iron Wolf, Black Death, and Green Legion already blooded, Samogitia now carried a true army of maneuver—six banners that could strike as one or wheel as three.

1453 — Peace, Renaissance, and an Imperial Crown

17 February: Denmark yielded ten years of reparations and 345 ducats; our ledger marked +2.0 prestige (Denmark −2.0). On 1 March we embraced the Renaissance; on 9 March we curtailed noble privileges. At last, on 14 April the bells out-rang the gulls: the sovereigns were crowned Emperor and Empress of Samogitia.

Armies Recast — August 1453

The veteran triad—Iron Wolf, Black Death, Green Legion—each expanded to 10k/6k/4k on 18 August. The field host thumped like a single drum.

1454 — Mass Drill & the Birth of a Navy

January 1454: the great training began—new manuals, new cadence, new steel. Then, on 8 May, masts rose in Klaipėda: 12 heavy ships crewed up under Mindaugas Olshanski, and the Royal Navy was born. The sea had been a border; now it was a road.

1456 — Steel and Oath Renewed

Change of command writes history in fine print. On 20 January Tarvydas Korybut passed from the rolls; Daugvilas Jogailaitis took Pagan Fear. In the same year, Royal Force and Iron Legion swelled to 10k/6k/4k, aligning with the triad of 1453. By autumn three flagships—Death, Fear, and Agony—made the roadstead quiet.

End-of-year tallies (1456): Army 55,000 / 33,000 / 22,000. Navy 16 / 31 / 51 / 20 (heavy / light / galley / transport).

1457 — Two Blades to the Sea

At first light on 1 January 1457 the navy split for reach and readiness: the Royal Navy under Mindaugas Olshanski remained at Klaipėda, bearing the flagship HMS Death, while the new Baltic Fleet under Aras Tyzenhaus sailed to Riga with HMS Fear and HMS Agony.
