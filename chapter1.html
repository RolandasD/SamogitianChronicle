Chapter 1: Dawn over Samogitia

1444 — The first formations

A Soldier’s Note, Winter 1444

We woke to frost on the cart-linens and a red sky over the pines. The bells of Medininkai counted Lauds, and the horses steamed like kettles. Marshal Tyzenhaus rode the line without a trumpet—only a nod for the captains, and for us a look that measured boots, straps, eyes.

They say great kingdoms live by their banners. We live by our boots. The Nemunas keeps our secrets; the forests keep our paths. When the Orders boast of their cannon, we sharpen axes and build causeways through marsh that would swallow an army with a single misstep.

Tonight the quartermaster writes that we are six thousand souls. Tomorrow the chaplain will say the number is seven thousand, for he counts the saints as well. I count the men beside me—Matas with the scar on his cheek, Jurgis who sings, Andrius who prays before he eats—and I think: if we must be an anvil, let us be the one that breaks hammers.

— Anonymous pikeman of the Medininkai watch, copied later into the Book of Campfires

Dawn breaks over pine and peat, over dunes that hold the sea at arm’s length and over river-mist that threads the valleys of Žemaitija. Traders on the Nemunas talk of empires; fishermen talk of weather; the court at Medininkai talks of survival. Between larger crowns and older Orders, Samogitia steps into the year 1444 with a small, disciplined heart.

“A young kingdom is a blade of grass in spring—soft in the hand, and yet with the strength to split stone.”
— Chronicle of Varniai, compiled c. 1480 (court scribe, anonymous)

The Land & the People

The heartland is forest-thick and water-latticed. To the west lie dunes and amber beaches; to the east, riverways and marsh-guarded villages where riders can vanish at a trot. The old fires of the Baltic faith still glow in customs and songs even as churches ring the hours—two histories braided into one people.

Grain is modest, timber is ample, iron is precious; horses are lean and enduring. Messengers range the forest-roads under banners stitched in the cloisters of Varniai and borne by hands that remember older gods. This is the temper of Samogitia—sinew before luxury, duty before display.

Order of Battle, 1444

The standing force totals 6,000 men: 5,000 infantry, 1,000 cavalry, and 0 artillery. It is a single field formation—tight, frugal, and ready—commanded by Daugvilas Tyzenhaus.

Doctrine & Drill

Without guns, siege is patience and persuasion. The infantry is the spine: shield-lines drilled to pivot around a standard as if around a hinge; pikes held like fences that walk forward. Cavalry rides light—farm-bred horses, leather tack, lances that can be repaired by a village smith. Supply is as careful as any catechism: rye for the men, oats for the mounts, salt for the winter. Tyzenhaus’ maxim is simple: “A small army must move as one mind.”

“We will be fewer than those who come for us. Then we must be earlier, steadier, and truer.”
— Daugvilas Tyzenhaus, instructions to captains, winter 1443/44

Position & Priorities

Hemmed in by larger neighbors and the Orders to the north and west, our early aims are practical and sharp: hold the heartland, keep the roads open to market towns, watch the coast and the river mouths, and keep the ledger balanced enough to feed the line through spring mud. We patrol borders not to provoke but to be counted.

Faces of the Field Army

Daugvilas Tyzenhaus walks the line before dawn, counting pikeheads the way a merchant counts coin. He is not beloved; he is trusted. Between sermons and saddle-sores, the captains learn his habits: strike into fog, never into glare; speak softly to scouts; eat last.

The rankers are sons of foresters and fishermen. They learn to cut fascines for marsh causeways, to lash sleighs under wagons when ruts freeze, to march by stars when clouds swallow the moon. A Samogitian soldier’s pay is lean and promptly delivered; his honor is the difference between a village that keeps its seed corn and one that does not.

“If we must be an anvil, then let us be the one that breaks hammers.”
— saying attributed to a Žemaitijan standard-bearer, recorded later in the Book of Campfires

Limits that Shape Us

  • No field artillery: walls are negotiated, not battered. Raids deny foraging; screens deny approach.
  • Every casualty counts: a small pool of trained men means retreats are tactical, not shameful.
  • Diplomacy is logistics by other means: a stable border feeds the army as surely as granaries do.

Signals of a Coming Name

In the sermons of the chaplain and the jokes of the farriers one hears a phrase begin to travel: Geležinis Vilkas, the Iron Wolf. The formation is not yet so named, but the men have already given it a shape in their stories. When the time comes to write banners, the words will already be waiting to be stitched.

“Name a thing for what you wish it to be, not for what it is.”
— Sister Agnė of Varniai, letter to the treasurer on the cost of new standards

Ledger & Lifeblood

The winter accounts show salt bought dear and horses shod twice. Timber goes out, flax comes in; the smith’s charcoal is the most carefully guarded pile in the town. We cannot afford pageantry; we can afford durability. What parades we keep are saved for the saints’ days and the day before marching.

Data, 1444: 6,000 total — 5,000 infantry, 1,000 cavalry, 0 artillery. One field army under Daugvilas Tyzenhaus, quartered from Medininkai. Siege capacity limited; doctrine favors maneuver and economy of force. The name “Iron Wolf” has not yet been granted—only spoken.
