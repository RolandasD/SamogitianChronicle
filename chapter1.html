Chapter I – The Dawn of Samogitia

Year 1444 — a candle flame defying the winds of empires.

Prologue

The year 1444 marked the birth of Samogitia’s fragile independence. Where once the land was a marcher province, battered between the ambitions of Lithuanians and Teutonic crusaders, a new ducal banner was raised in Žemaitija. The youth who bore it, Rolandas Drungilas, was scarcely fifteen. To many he seemed more a symbol than a sovereign — a flickering flame before Baltic storms. Yet that flame would kindle the idea of a realm determined to govern itself, no longer a pawn in the hands of others.

The State in 1444

Samogitia began with a single province, Žemaitija, a land of forests, rivers, and fiercely independent peasants. The ducal court was little more than a fortified manor surrounded by timber walls, but it carried weight: it was the first time a Samogitian ruler could style himself as duke in his own right, not as vassal. The nobles of the new court styled their house as Drungilas, claiming descent from warrior families who had resisted the crusader knights for generations.

The Iron Wolf

To defend the newborn duchy, a single host was raised: the Iron Wolf. Its name recalled the old legends of Vilnius, where a dream of a wolf howling like iron foretold the rise of a city. The Samogitians took the symbol for themselves, a reminder that even iron can sing when forged in fire.

The commander, Daugvilas Tyzenhaus, was a veteran of skirmishes against Livonian raiders. He drilled the infantry in shield‑wall and spear formations, while the cavalry remained the pride of Samogitian nobility. No artillery could be afforded, but the Iron Wolf’s reputation lay not in guns, but in the iron discipline of its ranks.

Perilous Diplomacy

Samogitia entered the stage without allies. To the west lay the Teutonic Order, still commanding castles and ports despite decades of decline. To the north, the Livonian Order eyed Žemaitija as unfinished business from the wars of the previous century. To the east, Lithuania — once overlord — regarded the young duchy with suspicion. Every border was a danger, and the Iron Wolf was both shield and bargaining chip in diplomacy.

Envoys carried words of peace, but behind them rode the knowledge that Samogitia was small. Its strength lay in stubbornness: its people had never bent easily to foreign swords.

Life in the Duchy

Beyond courts and campaigns, Samogitia in 1444 was a land of farmers and fishermen, villages clustered around wooden churches, and proud families clinging to traditions older than crowns. Oral tales of wolves, spirits, and rivers mixed with the rituals of Christianity recently planted by foreign missionaries. The duchy’s economy rested on grain, timber, and amber, traded in modest quantities with merchants from Riga and Prussia.

The new ducal household sought to centralize authority, but governance remained personal, negotiated with village elders and local boyars. Rule was fragile, but it carried legitimacy in the eyes of common folk: they called Rolandas “our duke” — not Lithuania’s, not the Order’s, but their own.
