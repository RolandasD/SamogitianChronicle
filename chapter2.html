Chapter 2: Iron Wolf

1445–1448 — Naming, expansion, War of the Orders, and elevation to a Kingdom

When the court named our field host Iron Wolf in the summer of 1445, it was more than ceremony. It was a promise: that Samogitia would not merely endure between taller shadows, but stand with its own.

1445 — The Naming of the Iron Wolf

The army grew to 10,000—5,000 infantry, 3,000 cavalry, and 2,000 artillery. Daugvilas Tyzenhaus drilled the line until it moved like a hinge; scouts ranged the river fords; smithies answered the cadence of marching feet.

1446 — A Second Claw, and a Son

In January 1446 the crown raised a second regiment, the Black Death, under Kantibutas Giedraiciai (5k/3k/2k). The realm, too, gained an heir: Rolandas, born 19 February (4/2/2), the very day of the royal marriage to Ausra. Fortune smiled on the house as the drill yards thundered.

In the wider theater, 26 April bound Poland and Lithuania in a personal union under Kazimierz Jagiellon (4/4/1)—with August as heir. Samogitia read the wind and, on 15 May, named Poland a rival. The board was set.

1447–1448 — War of the Orders

On 4 June 1447, banners unfurled and messengers rode: Samogitia declared war upon the Livonian Order and the Teutonic Order. Their muster drew in Mecklenburg, Riga, and Stettin. Our opening strength stood at 15,000 infantry, 9,000 cavalry, 6,000 artillery; the enemy gathered 25,000 infantry and 8,000 cavalry. Where they leaned on old walls, we trusted to new steel.

First Stroke — The Battle of Mitau (15 June 1447)

Tyzenhaus seized the crossings at dusk and struck before the Orders could cohere. With 10,000/6,000/4,000, Samogitia fell upon a divided host of 7,000/3,000/0. The fight was brief and terminal; enemy banners were trampled into the marsh-grass. Our line held perfect dressing—no casualties.

The Baltic Gate — Reval (24 August 1447)

To the north, Giedraiciai drove a light, fast column—5,000/3,000/2,000—through forest roads, catching a local garrison (1,000) before relief could ride. The gate to the sea opened with scarcely a scratch; again the enemy were annihilated.

Icewind River — Narwa (15 September 1447)

The same spearpoint swept east. On the banks at Narwa, the Black Death repeated the lesson: 5,000/3,000/2,000 against 1,000, the foe unmade, the road cleared.

Autumn Thunder — Stargard (23 November 1447)

As leaves burned gold, the Orders massed a truer force at Stargard—6,000/2,000/0. We met them with 5,000/3,000/2,000. The guns spoke; the horse rolled their flank. Victory cost us six souls, and the enemy once more was swept from the field.

Year’s End — Sieradz (31 December 1447)

Snow fell as the Green Legion proved its steel: 5,000/3,000/2,000 broke 6,000/3,000/0. Seven names entered the roll of honor; the foe’s roll ended.

Peace of 28 January 1448

The winter couriers carried terms to every court in the Baltic. The Livonian Order bowed to full annexation; Riga itself came to Samogitia; and from the Teutonic Order there fell to us Memel, Insterburg, Königsberg, Ermland, Ortelsburg, Marienburg, Danzig, Tuchel, Kulm, Ostroede. The treasury counted 170 ducats; prestige rose +9.2; the price in the balance of fear was a modest 5.1 AE and 0.01 inflation.

Crown & Culture (1448)

On 7 March 1448, amidst church-bells and the roar of the crowd, the sovereigns proclaimed the Kingdom of Samogitia. A month later, on 13 April, we laid claim to Baltic Cultural Hegemony: a pledge to steward the tongue and memory of the sea-lapped north. Diplomacy followed the banners—on 1 June, a Royal Marriage with Brandenburg bound new ties in iron and in ink.

Development Drive (1448–1449)

The hammers did not still when the drums ceased. Granaries widened, roads were metalled, and river quays deepened for broader keels. Guilds found patrons; the musters found grain. By the turn of 1449, the realm looked outward not with hunger, but with readiness.
