Chapter 2: Iron Wolf

1445–1448 — Naming, expansion, War of the Orders, and elevation to a Kingdom

Prologue: The Naming, Summer 1445

Cloister bells rolled across the parade square and the banners smelled of fresh dye. A hush took the ranks as the herald’s voice climbed the walls: Geležinis Vilkas—the Iron Wolf. The name landed like a mailed fist: not boast, but burden. Tyzenhaus said nothing. He only touched the standard once, as if testing the heat of iron.

That afternoon the line wheeled on a single beat of the drum, and the dust rose in a perfect crescent. The grooms swore the horses felt it too—the moment when men and mount moved like hinges of the same door.

— From the Chronicle of Varniai, “On the Banners of the Year 1445”

When the court named our field host Iron Wolf in the summer of 1445, it was more than ceremony. It was a promise: that Samogitia would not merely endure between taller shadows, but stand with its own.

“Name a thing not for what it is, but for what you demand it become.”
— Sister Agnė of Varniai to the treasurer, on the cost of new standards

1445 — The Naming of the Iron Wolf

The army grew to 10,000—5,000 infantry, 3,000 cavalry, and 2,000 artillery. Tyzenhaus drilled the line until it moved like a hinge; scouts sounded river fords; smithies beat a cadence that matched the march. Logistics were scripture: rye, oats, salt, and boots—always boots.

1446 — A Second Claw, and a Son

In January 1446 the crown raised a second regiment, the Black Death, under Kantibutas Giedraiciai (5k/3k/2k). The realm, too, gained an heir: Rolandas, born 19 February (4/2/2)—on the very day of the royal marriage to Ausra. Bells and bootsteps shared the streets; a dynasty and a drillbook turned their pages together.

On 26 April, Poland chose a Jagiellon and bound itself in a union with Lithuania under Kazimierz Jagiellon (4/4/1), with August as heir. Samogitia read the wind, and on 15 May named Poland a rival. The board was set; the pieces woke.

1447–1448 — War of the Orders

On 4 June 1447 the banners rose and the messengers rode. Samogitia declared war upon the Livonian Order and the Teutonic Order. Their muster drew in Mecklenburg, Riga, and Stettin. Our opening strength stood at 15,000 infantry, 9,000 cavalry, 6,000 artillery; the enemy gathered 25,000 infantry and 8,000 cavalry. They leaned on old walls. We trusted new steel.

First Stroke — The Battle of Mitau (15 June 1447)

Tyzenhaus seized the river crossings at dusk and struck before the Orders could cohere. With 10,000/6,000/4,000, Samogitia fell upon a divided host of 7,000/3,000/0. The fight was brief and terminal; enemy banners were trampled into marsh-grass. Our line held perfect dressing— no casualties. The Iron Wolf bared its teeth.

The Baltic Gate — Reval (24 August 1447)

Giedraiciai drove a fast column—5,000/3,000/2,000—through forest roads, catching a garrison of 1,000 before relief could ride. The gate to the sea opened with scarcely a scratch; the enemy were annihilated. A city’s harbor heard a new tongue shouting orders.

Icewind River — Narwa (15 September 1447)

The same spearpoint swept east. On the banks at Narwa the Black Death repeated the lesson: 5,000/3,000/2,000 against 1,000; the foe unmade, the road cleared. The columns slept with their boots on and their helmets for pillows.

Autumn Thunder — Stargard (23 November 1447)

As leaves burned gold, the Orders massed a truer force at Stargard—6,000/2,000/0. We met them with 5,000/3,000/2,000. The guns spoke; the horse rolled their flank. Victory cost us six souls; the enemy was swept from the field. Tyzenhaus’ note in the margin: “Courage is logistics arriving on time.”

Year’s End — Sieradz (31 December 1447)

Snow fell as the Green Legion proved its steel: 5,000/3,000/2,000 broke 6,000/3,000/0. Seven names entered the roll of honor; the foe’s roll ended. The year closed on iron.

Peace of 28 January 1448

Couriers carried terms through the winter dark. The Livonian Order bowed to full annexation; Riga itself came to Samogitia; and from the Teutonic Order there fell to us Memel, Insterburg, Königsberg, Ermland, Ortelsburg, Marienburg, Danzig, Tuchel, Kulm, Ostroede. The treasury counted 170 ducats; prestige rose +9.2; the price in the balance of fear was 5.1 AE and 0.01 inflation. The ledger closed; the maps changed.

Crown & Culture (1448)

On 7 March 1448, church-bells and crowd-roars announced the Kingdom of Samogitia. On 13 April, we claimed Baltic Cultural Hegemony—a promise to keep the sea’s tongue and memory. Diplomacy followed banners: 1 June sealed a Royal Marriage with Brandenburg. Quays deepened, roads were metalled, granaries widened; by year’s turn to 1449, our gaze was steady and forward.

“We built with timber, but we measured with time. Kingdoms are carpentry.”
— Guildmaster Stasys, on the great works after the war
